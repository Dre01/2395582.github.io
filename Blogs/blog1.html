‘As We May Think’ at 65

“Come writers and critics Who prophesize with your pen.” - Bob Dylan.

The prophetic nature of ‘As We May Think’ is hard to miss. It’s certainly not lost on Harper who does admit that the piece has reached "pensionable age" but goes on to rhetorically ask; "... why does this work still continue to resonate 65 years after it was first published?" To think of an article published in the 40s being a precursor to the World Wide Web (Ohhhh so that’s what WWW stands for), seems a bit strange at first, but the predictive element in the piece cannot be denied. Hence the Bob Dylan quote.

Harper’s dissection of the then 65-year-old piece, focuses mainly on Bush’s thoughts on information gathering through association, rather than through an abstract, hierarchical structure. This is because of the insistence that the human brain, works through association; one thought leads to the next, then to the next and so on. The logical step, then is that if information is to be provided to the human brain it should be in a way that follows this method of associative learning. This would result in better retention and understanding of information, which is ultimately the goal.

However this notion, while very romantic is not exactly viable. As a result we have been forced to revert back to hierarchical structures. This is because of the vast amounts of information and the vast amounts of people perusing it. It becomes quite difficult to cater to the individual when there are so many individuals.

As a result we have a system which has ridiculously vast amounts of information to dispel in a way that is not entirely conducive to an exercise in genuine information gathering. If the information can not be learned in a way that is better to understand, than understanding, most likely, will not come.

What I’ve decided to take from this reading, or rather couldn’t help but take form this reading is that the way we provide the information is almost as important as the information itself. Which is why a definite effort is required in innovating in this field and attempting to fulfill what Bush regarded our mission – “… it is the privilege of man to learn to understand, and that this is his mission.”

I would like to end with a quote from the piece: “Personal associations derived from experience … enable us to learn better and understand better.”
